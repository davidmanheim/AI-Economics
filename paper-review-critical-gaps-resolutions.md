## C1 — Proposition 4 / `prop:breakthrough` (statement ~707–723, proposition ~747–763; proof in Appendix F ~1159–1251)

**What changed.** The proposition and its proof were restructured into two explicitly separate parts, proven in two different, clearly labeled models, rather than one proposition proven by a single (flawed) argument:

- **Part 1 (markup and elasticity).** Proven under Bertrand price competition in the slack-capacity regime, with a *general* compute-inclusive marginal cost `κ ≥ 0` (not the old `κ=0` normalization). Appendix F, §"Bertrand equilibrium and comparative statics (slack capacity)" (~line 1183, `subsec:app-bertrand`) derives closed forms `μ_L = Δ(2Q_L−κ)/(3Q_L+Δ)` and `ε_LL = [Q_L/(2Q_L−κ)]·(2+3κ/Δ)` where `Δ≡Q_L−Q_F`, and differentiates both directly: `∂μ_L/∂Δ = (2Q_L−κ)·3Q_L/(3Q_L+Δ)² > 0` whenever `2Q_L>κ` (i.e., whenever `μ_L>0`, the proposition's own interior-equilibrium condition), and `∂ε_LL/∂Δ = −3κQ_L/[(2Q_L−κ)Δ²] < 0` whenever `κ>0`. This replaces "can be verified directly" with an actual derivative, and resolves the vacuous-elasticity problem honestly: at `κ=0` the derivative is identically zero and `ε_LL≡1`, which the text now states is the *degenerate boundary* of the result, not a demonstration of it.
- **Part 2 (differential compute rent).** The flawed capacity-free Bertrand "bridging paragraph" (old line 1172) is deleted and replaced with a new subsection, "Capacity-constrained Cournot and the compute-rent comparative static" (~line 1219, `subsec:app-cournot-breakthrough`). This builds a model in which `x_ℓ` genuinely appears: laboratories precommit compute `x_ℓ`, deploy it as output `y_ℓ=x_ℓ/a_ℓ`, and receive the two-model system's price — the Kreps–Scheinkman reduced form (capacity-then-price competition ⟹ Cournot outcomes) already cited in the literature review, now actually used rather than gestured at. `v̂_ℓ(x_ℓ,x_{-ℓ}) ≡ ∂π_ℓ*/∂x_ℓ` is then a well-defined function of the compute allocation, evaluated at the winner-take-all corner `x_L=K, x_F=0` (the corner the paper's own `r*≃max{c,v̂_F}` definition already presumes). Closed form: `v̂_L−v̂_F = −K·Q_L + Δ·(1−K)`, so `∂(v̂_L−v̂_F)/∂Δ = 1−K > 0` whenever `K<1`, which is exactly the paper's maintained scarcity condition (capacity insufficient to serve the whole addressable market), not an extra assumption. The formula also self-disciplines the corner: at `Δ=0` it gives `v̂_L−v̂_F=−KQ_L<0`, correctly saying the corner should *not* arise absent a quality gap.
- The proposition statement (~line 747) now states both parts with their respective scope conditions, and explicitly flags that Part 2 holds `κ` fixed as `Δ` varies (the review's point 3), with a paragraph explaining why the partial-equilibrium version is still informative (isolates the direct/mechanical channel from the indirect wholesale-price channel; approximates the impact effect over the short window the empirical predictions target).

**What is now proven vs. still assumed.** Both halves are now proven by direct differentiation in named, fully specified models (Bertrand/slack-capacity for Part 1; capacity-constrained Cournot/binding-capacity for Part 2), each restricted to the two-model demand system of Lemma 1. ## Follow-up: fixed-cost/rival-type asymmetry and the subscription-friction relationship in Proposition 3

Follow-up round on the C4 fix, prompted by the author's observation that the friction-only protection story ignores the huge fixed cost for labs (so the competitive position of a potential rival depends on whether it has already paid `F`), and that there is an unresolved relationship between subscription models and friction. Paper recompiles cleanly after these edits (50 pages, no errors, no undefined references; the four remaining overfull-hbox warnings are pre-existing and outside the edited regions).

### A correction found along the way: the C4 threshold `s_min = BK/a - r*` was wrong

Before addressing the two new problems, checking the prior fix's derivation against an independently computed contested-segment equilibrium exposed a unit error in the C4 pass. The old derivation computed the rival's per-output margin **net** of compute cost, `(P* - s) - kappa` with `kappa = b + a*r*`, and then compared that against the per-FLOP price `r*` -- double-counting the compute cost and mixing per-output with per-FLOP quantities. The object Proposition 1's protection condition needs is the rival's **gross** marginal value per FLOP, `v_rival,k(0) = (P* - s - b)/a`, and `v <= r*` rearranges to

```
s >= s_min = P* - b - a*r* = B*K/a   (the full segment markup; not BK/a - r*)
```

The corrected threshold is confirmed independently by the new contested-segment equilibrium (below): an entrant's post-entry profit is strictly positive for every `s < BK/a` and hits zero exactly at `s = BK/a`. Numerical check (A=10, b=1, a=2, B=0.5, K=6, S=3): old `s_min = -1.5` claimed self-protection at `s=0`, yet the contested equilibrium at `s=0` gives the rival flow profit 0.54 > 0 -- the old condition demonstrably failed to protect. Consequences of the correction, now in the text (`eq:friction-threshold` and the derivation paragraph after the proof):

- `s_min = BK/a` is the **entire markup** the niche sustains -- ordinary contestability logic: a quality-parity rival produces at the same compute-inclusive cost, so protecting a margin requires a friction at least as large as the margin.
- The old "where `s_min <= 0` the configuration protects itself without any friction" claim is deleted; under quality parity the segmented configuration is **never** self-protecting (`s_min > 0` whenever the markup is positive), and the required friction scales one-for-one with the profitability of the niche it defends.
- A "watch the units" parenthetical is left in the text naming the incorrect comparison explicitly, so the error cannot silently reappear.
- With the corrected threshold, the deviation condition and the equilibrium condition are exact complements (below `s_min` the segmented configuration is not an equilibrium and a contested one exists; at or above it, the reverse) -- no window of equilibrium multiplicity, which the wrong threshold would have implied.

### Problem A -- two structurally different rivals

**(1) Does the s_min derivation cover incumbent-vs-incumbent invasion? Yes -- verbatim, and only that.** The derivation prices a rival's first unit at compute opportunity cost `r*` under quality parity, and nothing else: it therefore applies literally to the other S-1 assigned laboratories (training cost sunk; compute fungible across segments; cross-segment separability means invasion leaves home-segment profits untouched). `F` appears nowhere in it -- fixed training costs provide **no** protection against already-trained rivals; the friction is the entire defense. Symmetrically, the derivation is irrelevant to a lab with no trained model, which cannot supply a first unit at any friction level without paying `F` first. This two-rival-type structure is now stated explicitly in a new part (4) of Proposition 3 and a scope paragraph after the derivation.

**(2) New derivation: fresh entrants are deterred by `F` alone, no friction needed, in a nonempty range.** New part (4) with proof paragraph. A quality-parity entrant that pays `F` and contests one segment (Cournot in the contested segment, price-taking in `r`, capacity binding -- the paper's maintained conduct) earns post-entry flow profit

```
pi^E(S,s) = 4S(BK/a - s)^2 / (B(3S+1)^2),   pi^E(S,0) = [4S^2/(3S+1)^2]*pi^seg(S) < (4/9)*pi^seg(S)
```

(derivation: monopolists supply D/(2SB), contested-segment duopolists (D+s)/(3SB) and (D-2s)/(3SB) with D = A-b-ar, capacity clearing gives D = (6SBK/a + 2s)/(3S+1); verified numerically, FOCs and capacity residuals at machine precision). So fresh entry into an occupied segment is strictly unprofitable at any friction level, including s = 0, whenever `(rho+Lambda)F > pi^E(S,0)`; since `pi^E(S,0) < pi^seg(S)`, the range of fixed costs where the S incumbents are viable (part 3) while every fresh entrant is deterred friction-free is nonempty for every S. Side results recorded in the proof paragraph: entry *raises* the wholesale price (partly self-defeating for the entrant, mirroring Prop 5(4)'s market-growth logic); capacity still binds post-entry on the proposition's whole domain (`D <= 2BK/a < A-b-ac`); `pi^E` vanishing exactly at `s = BK/a` verifies the corrected `s_min` from the equilibrium side.

**(3) The sharper version -- why S labs rather than one lab spanning all segments: resolved as an explicit limitation, not papered over.** The old "countervailing force" paragraph (incumbents "invade an adjacent segment at no incremental training cost") was doubly wrong after the C4 fix: under the friction condition, marginal invasion is exactly what is blocked, so the paragraph contradicted the proposition it followed. The rewritten paragraph now says: (i) post-assignment, the S-lab configuration IS stable against incumbent cross-invasion -- that is precisely what `s >= s_min` delivers, and it is derived, not assumed; (ii) the economies-of-scope force survives at the *assignment stage*, which the model does not represent: nothing explains why S labs each pay `F` rather than one lab paying `F` once and becoming the assigned (friction-free) provider of every segment. A spanning lab earns `S*pi^seg(S) = BK^2/a^2` per period -- the head-on monopoly profit `pi(1)` -- for one training cost, is protected against fresh entrants by exactly the same part-(4) condition (price-taking in `r*` makes its FOCs segment-separable, so the contested-segment equilibrium is unchanged), and no purchaser bears the friction on path. Checked against the model's primitives for a reason this wouldn't happen: none exists -- quality is at parity by assumption, `chi_{l,k}` is an endowment rather than a per-segment investment, and `s_imk` is a utility primitive attached to an assignment taken as given. What would break the spanning lab's dominance is a per-segment cost of *becoming* a segment's friction-free provider (segment-specific integration/distribution/fine-tuning investment), which the model does not contain as a choice variable. Conclusion now stated in the proposition (part 3 pointer), the discussion, and `subsec:entry-honesty`: `S-bar` is a **viability ceiling, not an equilibrium count** -- every configuration from one spanning lab to S-bar one-segment labs is stable and viable under the stated conditions, and the model does not select among them.

### Problem B -- friction vs. subscriptions: `s` and `E_ij` are different primitives, now said everywhere it matters

Worked out the relationship: `s_imk` enters utility purchase by purchase, so as used in the model (and as required by the `s_min` derivation, which subtracts `s` from the rival's price on every unit) it is a **recurring, per-output** cost (latency, privacy, per-task contractual/compliance overhead). `E_ij` in the subscription appendix is a **one-time, relationship-level** cost, paid once to add a provider to a portfolio, after which routing is frictionless -- a genuinely different primitive, not the same object. A one-time cost cannot satisfy `eq:friction-threshold` for two reasons, both now in the text: it *amortizes* (per-output equivalent `E/T` for a purchaser with discounted volume `T`, negligible for exactly the high-volume purchasers who dominate compute demand) and it is *extinguished by multi-homing* (once a cross-segment purchaser pays `E_ij` under the appendix's incremental-value condition, no wedge remains on routing segment-k tasks to the rival). Also now stated: Proposition 3's partition of purchasers into S disjoint segments quietly assumes single-homing -- every purchaser has tasks in one segment only, so the appendix's multi-homing mechanism and Section 6.1's persistent-multi-homing prediction are switched off by construction. The honest scope statement, in the discussion and in `subsec:entry-honesty`: segment protection is a claim about single-homed purchasers facing recurring frictions, eroding as multi-homing spreads. One additional connection (the author's parenthetical about subscription models): the per-period subscription fee `phi_j` is itself a recurring relationship-level cost, so subscription pricing endogenously generates a friction-like wedge -- but amortizing like `phi_j/T`, protecting niches against low-volume purchasers while doing little against high-volume ones.

Edits supporting this: Section 6.1's introduction of `s_imk` now flags the recurring/one-time decomposition at the outset (the old informal list "an integration cost ... a switching cost" mixed the two) with a forward pointer to Prop 3; the persistent-multi-homing paragraph now assigns the division of labor (`E_ij` governs portfolio entry, recurring `s_imk` governs routing stickiness); the appendix's definition of `E_ij` now carries the reverse pointer; Prop 3's statement requires the recurring component explicitly.

### What is now proven vs. still open

**Proven (new this round):** the corrected protection threshold `s_min = BK/a` (derived two independent ways -- deviation pricing and the contested-segment equilibrium -- which now agree exactly); the two-rival-type dichotomy (friction is the entire defense against trained rivals, `F` the entire defense against untrained ones); the closed-form entrant profit `pi^E(S,s)` and the friction-free fresh-entry deterrence range; post-entry capacity binding on the proposition's whole domain; the spanning-lab profit `S*pi^seg(S) = pi(1)` and its equal protection.

**Still open, now stated as limitations rather than left implicit:** (i) the assignment/selection stage -- no mechanism predicts S-bar separate labs over one spanning lab (or anything between); would require a per-segment cost of acquiring assigned-provider status, absent from the model; (ii) protection covers only single-homed purchasers under recurring frictions -- no result about equilibrium erosion dynamics as multi-homing spreads; (iii) as before, no claim that real-world frictions reach `s_min` (now a *harder* bar than the erroneous earlier one: the full segment markup), and the assignment itself remains a primitive.

### What the new appendix contains (six subsections), derived vs. qualitative vs. open

- **`subsec:slack-condition` (When is capacity slack?).** *Derived/collected:* the slack condition is conduct-dependent -- integrated-benchmark version (negation of Sec 4's scarcity assumption) vs. oligopoly version (Sec 7's) -- and in the linear system the thresholds are ordered (1/2)K-bar (segmented) < n/(n+1)K-bar (Cournot-n) < K-bar = a(A-b-ac)/B (price-taking), pulling together eq:cournot-slack-threshold and the segmented threshold already scattered in the paper. Three recorded consequences: (i) an "oligopoly-slack but competitively-scarce" window where idle FLOPs coexist with unserved above-cost willingness to pay (output withheld by market power, not rationed by hardware); (ii) the boundary is endogenous to n, so entry alone can re-bind a slack market; (iii) r* reaches c continuously at the threshold -- the regimes join without a jump.
- **`subsec:slack-cournot` (Cournot at r* = c).** *Derived:* standard unconstrained Cournot in the paper's own linear system: P-slack = b+ac+(A-b-ac)/(n+1), the three-component price decomposition with the scarcity-rent component identically zero, and a side-by-side table (`tab:regime-contrast`) making the contrast with prop:capacity-neutrality the spine: binding price depends on K not n, slack price on n not K; the policy levers (build GPUs vs. add laboratories) swap roles at the boundary; price is continuous and kinked in K. *Honestly flagged:* exactness is specific to the symmetric homogeneous linear system.
- **`subsec:slack-free-entry` (Free entry).** *Derived:* the full derivation behind Prop 5(4)'s one-line aside -- pi-slack(n) = (A-b-ac)^2/(B(n+1)^2), free-entry count n*+1 = floor((A-b-ac)/sqrt(B(rho+Lambda)F)), and with the uniform-theta microfoundation the count rises proportionally to sqrt(N) (the aggregate slack profit pool scales linearly with N because B ~ 1/N -- Sutton's logic unobstructed -- versus the binding pool that shrinks with N). *Also derived (new):* flow profit is min{pi-bind, pi-slack} at every (n,K) with continuity at the threshold, so the fully consistent free-entry count is min{n*_bind, n*_slack} -- resolving the n-depends-on-regime/regime-depends-on-n circularity with no cycling and no gap. Corollary recorded with scope plainly stated: holding K fixed, the equilibrium count is single-peaked in market size N, maximized near the regime boundary (linear-example property, not a general theorem).
- **`subsec:slack-investment` (Investment near the boundary).** *Derived:* lambda(K) is linear, strictly decreasing, and hits zero exactly at the threshold; in a deterministic stationary environment with depreciation, a slack steady state contradicts the Euler condition (zero rents vs. positive marginal capex), so any stationary equilibrium sits strictly inside the binding region with lambda_ss equal to amortized capacity cost -- Boiteux/Crew-Kleindorfer peak-load logic recovered from the model's own investment condition. *Qualitative (labeled as such):* "overshoot is unlikely to be planned" -- but transient slack episodes are fully consistent (T_build lag, demand uncertainty, efficiency shocks are the bad tail of ex ante sensible investment), and three defeaters sit outside the model (strategic hyperscaler investment races, captive/integrated demand, non-rent motives). Explicit closing status line: lambda vanishing is derived; planned-overshoot-unlikely is qualitative; overshoot-won't-happen is not claimed.
- **`subsec:slack-niches` (Niche protection without scarcity).** *Derived:* rerunning Prop 6's three-line derivation at r = c gives segment monopoly price b+ac+(A-b-ac)/2 and protection threshold s >= (A-b-ac)/2 (the unconstrained monopoly markup), confirmed from the entrant side by the contested-segment equilibrium (entrant profit vanishes exactly at that s). Required friction is continuous in K, rising on the binding domain (BK/a) and plateauing at the slack value -- so compute scarcity was itself doing part of the protective work and capacity growth erodes niche protection *before* the regime flips. Segmentation stops being price-neutral under slack (monopoly price strictly above head-on Cournot for S >= 2): market division now has the ordinary welfare cost that capacity-pinned output previously suppressed. Viability ceiling analog S-bar-slack = floor((A-b-ac)^2/(4B(rho+Lambda)F)). *Open (stated):* the required friction (half the choke-price margin) is even more demanding than the binding case and unjustified by anything in the paper; without it, parity + slack is textbook homogeneous-good Bertrand and no niche survives; whether taste-based horizontal differentiation replaces scarcity protection is outside the paper's primitives and left genuinely open; the assignment-selection gap carries over verbatim.
- **`subsec:slack-empirics` (Observable markers).** Direct marker: the wholesale margin r*-c (rental prices at operating cost = no capital recovery) plus utilization; indirect markers: the table's comparative statics (which variable consumer prices respond to). Points to the new empirical prediction 5. Transition drivers on both sides identified, including that entry pushes *toward* binding. Two explicit non-claims: the single-peaked count is not stated as an empirical prediction, and nothing forecasts which regime the industry occupies at any horizon.
